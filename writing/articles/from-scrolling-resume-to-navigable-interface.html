The first version of my portfolio worked well enough to publish. It showed me that the main problem was not missing content. The harder problem was deciding what a visitor should see first and how quickly they could reach the rest.

The important information was there, but the layout still read like a long résumé. Someone looking for projects had to move through much of the same page as someone checking my recent experience or trying to contact me.

What began as a visual redesign became an exercise in UX design and information triage. I needed to decide what belonged in the first view, what could wait, and how the page should guide different visitors.

The first version revealed the problem

Publishing the original site gave me something more useful than another mockup. I could finally judge how the full page felt when someone used it from beginning to end.

The site already covered my background and work. It also had a consistent visual direction and working responsive behavior. The problems only became obvious once those pieces were assembled into one page.

Some information appeared more than once. Projects and employment details sat farther down the page than they needed to. The same card style also appeared across sections that served very different purposes.

The first version was therefore not work that needed to be discarded. It became the prototype that showed me what to redesign.

Scrolling made the visitor do the organizing

The original portfolio followed a familiar structure. It began with an introduction and continued downward through separate sections.

That structure was simple, but it gave the page control over the order in which everything was discovered. A visitor interested in one subject still had to locate it within the full sequence.

The repeated cards made that problem harder to notice. Skills, employers, projects, and contact actions were visually separated, but they often received similar weight. The page looked organized without clearly showing which information mattered in each context.

Large containers also consumed more space than the content required. The result was a page that felt longer without necessarily becoming more informative.

The redesign needed to do more than shorten the page. It needed to make the route through the information clearer.

Design around what the visitor wants

The main change was replacing one fixed reading order with several direct paths.

The site navigation still moves between major destinations. Inside the homepage, the Career Snapshot lets a visitor switch between the overall summary, professional experience, projects, and credentials.

The two navigation levels have separate jobs. The main navigation changes where the visitor is on the site. The Career Snapshot changes what they are examining within the homepage.

The Overview provides enough context for a quick visit. It introduces my role and shows the main areas of my work without requiring every tab to be opened.

A visitor who wants more detail can choose the relevant view immediately. The summary no longer has to contain the complete story.

Give each type of information the right structure

The original page reused cards because they were consistent and easy to build. The redesign kept a shared visual language but stopped treating every kind of information the same way.

Keep experience chronological

Employment history is easiest to understand when each role follows the same structure. The visitor can compare time periods and responsibilities without learning a different layout for every employer.

Let project depth shape the layout

Projects do not all have the same amount of evidence behind them. A system with working screenshots and a deeper case study can reasonably receive more space than an earlier prototype.

Keep supporting information compact

Credentials matter, but they do not need the same narrative space as employment or project work. A compact ledger keeps them easy to find without turning them into the focus of the page.

Contact information has a more practical purpose. Keeping it in the side rail makes the next action available without interrupting the main story.

I initially tried giving the NVIDIA experience record much more space than the other employers. That approach worked for featured projects, so it seemed like a reasonable way to emphasize my most relevant role.

In practice, the layout weakened the career timeline and left awkward empty space. Employment needed consistency more than visual emphasis.

The asymmetric layout remained useful for projects, where differences in scope are meaningful. The experience view returned to a consistent chronological structure.

Create clarity without adding more noise

Fitting more information into the first screen did not automatically make the page easier to use. Some iterations met the height target only because the text became too small.

Other versions added more borders and labels in an attempt to clarify the structure. Instead, they made the interface feel busier.

The page improved when the layout began doing more of the explanatory work. Alignment showed which items belonged together. Spacing separated subjects without placing each one inside a large card.

The visual styling also became quieter. Cyan now marks interaction and state instead of covering every border. Animation is used to confirm a change rather than making the page constantly move.

Mobile required a separate decision rather than a scaled-down desktop layout. The smaller version needs a clear reading order and controls large enough to use comfortably.

The lesson was simple: structure should create clarity before decoration is added.

AI made iteration faster, not automatic

ChatGPT and coding agents shortened the time between describing an idea and seeing it work in the browser. That made it easier to compare layouts through real screenshots instead of debating them in the abstract.

The faster loop was especially useful when a change affected several parts of the site. I could revise a layout, check for regressions, and correct related styling within the same session.

Speed did not determine whether a result was good. A coding agent could enlarge a card or reduce the text size, but it could not decide whether the page became easier to understand.

Rejected versions remained useful because they exposed the wrong assumptions. The oversized employer card showed that experience needed a timeline. Crowded panels showed that additional labels were not always helpful.

AI also made unnecessary iteration easier. Once another variation became cheap to produce, deciding when to stop became part of the design work.

The tool accelerated implementation. The responsibility for framing the problem and judging the result remained mine.

The redesign still needs real evidence

A clearer layout does not prove that every visitor will understand it immediately. The current version represents my best explanation of how the information should be organized.

The first question is whether a new visitor understands my role without needing to explore the entire site. The next is whether the Career Snapshot tabs are noticeable and distinct from the main navigation.

Accessibility also needs direct review. Keyboard behavior and screen-reader structure should match what the page communicates visually.

Mobile testing must confirm that the reading order remains natural and that every control is comfortable to use. Performance will matter more as the site gains additional images and longer case studies.

Those questions require observation and measurement. They cannot be answered by the design looking finished.

The portfolio became easier to extend

The redesign reduced the need to keep lengthening the homepage whenever new work was added. The summary can remain compact while dedicated pages carry the deeper material.

The Projects page demonstrates how the design can expand around technical work without forcing every project into the homepage.

The Writing archive follows the same principle. It feels connected to the rest of the site while using a structure designed for reading.

The first portfolio displayed my information. The current version gives visitors clearer choices about where to begin and how far they want to explore.

The next stage should focus on stronger project evidence rather than another complete visual redesign. Working demonstrations and measured results will add more value than another layer of styling.

The most important change was not how the portfolio looked. It was making the information easier to reach and easier to understand.
