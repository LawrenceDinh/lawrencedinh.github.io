Lately, when I have a free hour, I am less likely to start a game. I am more likely to open ChatGPT or Codex.

I might start several agents, plan a project, review what they changed, test the result, and decide what to improve next. The session quickly builds momentum.

Each result gives me something new to react to. A good result makes me want to improve it. A bad result gives me a problem to solve. Either way, there is often a reason to continue.

The output is real, which makes the time feel productive. That also makes the loop harder to judge. I may be building something useful, but I can still continue because another result feels close or another problem appears solvable.

This has started to replace some of the time I once spent gaming. It made me wonder whether AI could become more than a work tool.

Games hold attention through control, feedback, progress, surprise, and the feeling that the next result is close. AI can offer many of the same things.

It can do this in three main ways:

People can watch or listen to generated media. They can direct AI characters, agents, and machines. They can also use AI to build tools, media, games, and other systems.

Not everyone will choose to create. Most people may prefer to watch, play, or direct. But for people who have ideas and enjoy building, AI can make creation feel much closer to play.

Why games hold our attention

Games give people control inside a responsive world. The player takes an action and quickly sees what happens.

A player can move, explore, build, fail, improve, and try again. Failure usually has a low cost. Progress is easy to see. The game teaches the player how its systems work through repeated feedback.

GTA, Minecraft, and Roblox show different forms of this control.

GTA emphasizes freedom inside an open world. GTA Online also provides creator tools for community-built missions and other activities. Minecraft supports open-ended play, building, experimentation, and a Creative mode focused on creating with blocks. Roblox lets users create, test, publish, and make experiences available to other users. [1, 2, 3]

These games show that people do not only enjoy winning. They also enjoy changing a world, testing ideas, and seeing how a system reacts.

Games make this easy because someone else has already built the world and its rules. The player can enter the system and begin acting right away.

Creating something useful outside a game has usually been harder. It may require technical knowledge, tools, setup, money, and a clear plan. AI is lowering some of those barriers.

AI offers three forms of entertainment

Consume

AI can generate videos, images, voices, music, stories, and characters. People do not need to build anything to enjoy this content. They can watch, listen, scroll, or share it.

The supply is growing quickly, although high production does not always mean high demand. In March 2026, TikTok said its AI-content labeling efforts had helped label more than 1.3 billion videos. [4] In April 2026, Deezer said it was receiving almost 75,000 fully AI-generated tracks each day, about 44 percent of its daily uploads. Those tracks received between one and three percent of streams on Deezer. [5] AI media is adding a large new supply of content, but people are not paying equal attention to all of it.

This part of AI competes with games in the same way that social media, streaming video, and music compete with games. It gives people another source of entertainment that can fill their free time.

Direct

AI can also give people something to control.

A person can tell a chatbot to create a story, ask a character to act a certain way, send an agent to complete a task, or change the direction of a generated experience.

This is closer to playing a game than watching a video. The user provides a goal, the system responds, and the user decides what should happen next.

Natural language may replace some of the buttons, menus, and controller inputs used in games. Instead of learning a complex control system, the user can explain the result they want.

This could eventually include physical machines. A person may be able to tell a robot where to go or what task to complete without building the robot or writing its software.

Create

AI can also help people build something new.

This is the path that interests me most. I can use AI to create a website, software tool, article, automation, data system, game, or hardware prototype.

The result can remain useful after the session ends. A game item usually stays inside the game. A website, script, dashboard, or automated tool can keep working outside the session.

Creation requires more effort than watching content or directing a finished system. The user still needs an idea. They need to decide what looks good, what works, and what should be changed.

AI makes the work easier to begin, but it does not provide curiosity, judgment, or direction on its own.

Why the AI loop keeps moving

Submitting a prompt creates anticipation. The user knows what they asked for, but they do not know exactly what the model will produce.

A good result feels satisfying. It may solve the problem, look better than expected, or turn an idea into something real.

A bad result can also keep the session going. It creates a new question: why did the AI misunderstand the request, and how can the instruction be improved?

Dopamine plays a role in learning, motivation, and reward processing. Research on reward prediction error examines the difference between a predicted reward and the reward that is actually received. A better-than-expected result can produce a positive prediction error and help shape future learning. [6]

The cited research did not study AI use. Applying this general mechanism to AI interaction is my interpretation, and it does not mean AI affects every person in the same way. It helps explain why an unexpectedly good result can make another attempt feel worth trying.

The loop can hold attention in different ways. It can support deep focus on one problem, but it can also create rapid switching between agents, outputs, tests, and corrections. Both feel active, but they are not always the same kind of attention.

The gacha-like part of AI

AI generation has some similarities to gacha systems, but the comparison has limits.

In a gacha game, the player performs an action and receives an uncertain reward. Most results may be common, while a rare result feels more exciting.

AI can have a similar reveal. A response may be weak, average, useful, or much better than expected. In open-ended tasks, results can vary because of the prompt, the selected model, and variation within the model's generation process. [7]

The main difference is control.

A gacha player usually has little control over the result. An AI user can improve the chance of a useful result by adding context, examples, limits, screenshots, files, or clearer instructions.

This can be called steerable uncertainty. The result remains uncertain, but the user can guide it.

When productive momentum becomes hard to judge

The difficult part is that productive and compulsive use can look similar. I can spend a long session improving a real project and finish with new features, files, or working software. That makes the time easier to justify than several hours spent gaming. But useful output does not automatically mean every extra hour is useful.

Running several agents also changes the shape of my attention. Instead of following one task from beginning to end, I move between prompts, results, tests, screenshots, and corrections. I stay active, but my attention can become fragmented. Many things are moving at once, which can feel like progress even when much of my time is spent supervising unfinished work.

This creates a harder question: am I choosing creation over consumption, or turning creation into a more defensible attention loop? I may end the session with real output, but I still need to judge whether I improved the underlying system or only made more changes.

When creation becomes leisure

Programming, design, writing, electronics, and other forms of making have always been enjoyable for some people.

They offer many of the same things found in games:

  • clear goals;
  • problems to solve;
  • visible progress;
  • new skills;
  • surprising results;
  • the freedom to choose what happens next.

The main problem was the barrier to starting.

A new project might require someone to learn a programming language, install tools, configure an environment, read documentation, and solve setup problems before seeing a useful result.

AI can move the first visible result closer to the beginning.

A person with a clear idea and a sense of design may now be able to create a basic working product through natural language.

Someone who understands how a website should look and behave may direct an early version without knowing every detail of HTML, CSS, or JavaScript. Someone who understands a work process may create an automation before learning every part of an API.

AI can help someone reach a first working version. More knowledge is still needed to make that version secure, reliable, fast, and easy to maintain.

AI lowers the starting barrier. It does not replace judgment, testing, technical understanding, direction, or responsibility.

For some people, this faster loop can turn development into leisure. They may open an AI tool for the same reason they once opened a game. They want to explore an idea, improve a system, and see what they can make.

Debugging the AI's actions

AI does not remove debugging. It changes what needs to be debugged.

Sometimes the code is broken. Other times, the code works but the AI solved the wrong problem.

The AI may have misunderstood the goal. It may have read the wrong file, missed important context, selected the wrong tool, or made an incorrect assumption.

The user must then debug the AI's behavior. They need to work out why it acted that way and how to give it better direction.

This requires more than writing a short prompt. The user must:

  • define the goal;
  • provide the right context;
  • recognize what is wrong;
  • break large tasks into smaller steps;
  • check the result;
  • watch for new problems and regressions.

The user becomes a director, tester, and behavior debugger.

This can also make the process engaging. A failed result becomes a puzzle. Fixing it creates the same kind of progress that comes from solving a difficult problem in a game.

Most people will direct rather than create

Easier tools will not turn everyone into a maker.

Creating something still requires initiative. The user needs an idea, a reason to continue, and the ability to make decisions. They also need enough patience to improve a weak result.

Most people may prefer to watch generated media or direct finished AI systems. That requires less effort than starting a project from nothing.

This is already how games work. A smaller group builds the game, while a much larger group plays it. AI may increase the number of creators, but creators will probably remain a smaller part of the total audience.

At the same time, AI may give regular users more control over finished experiences. A person may ask for a different story, character, goal, environment, or style without building the full system.

AI can therefore compete with games from both sides. It can give large audiences more personal entertainment, and it can give creators faster ways to build new experiences.

When AI control enters the physical world

The same directing model could eventually extend from digital agents to physical robots. A future owner may not need to build the machine or write its control software. They may give a goal to a prebuilt system, watch what it does, and redirect it when needed.

Humanoid robots are still early and expensive, but some models are now sold directly. Unitree currently lists the G1 at $13,500 and the R1 starting at $4,900. [8] Unitree also warns that the humanoid industry remains in an early stage and tells users to keep a safe distance from the machine during operation. [9]

Virtual or augmented reality could make this feel closer to controlling a game character. The difference is that the character would move through the physical world, where mistakes have real safety and privacy costs.

This extends the same attention loop: request, action, observation, and correction. Some people will build these systems, but many more may simply direct them.

What could slow this shift

AI will not replace every reason people play games.

Games provide stories, competition, social spaces, clear rules, and carefully designed challenges. Many people also play because they want to relax. They may not want to manage another project after work.

Generated media can become repetitive. AI can produce wrong or weak results. Making can also become frustrating when progress slows down.

A person can spend hours making changes without improving the underlying result. A large number of edits can look like progress even when the main problem remains. Faster output makes it easier to stay busy, but it does not guarantee better work.

This connects back to the attention problem. A useful project can still contain wasteful loops. The user has to decide whether the next change improves the system or only keeps the session moving.

Physical robots create larger concerns. They can cause harm, collect private information, or act in unsafe ways. These risks will limit how quickly personal robots become normal.

AI may compete with gaming without replacing it. People will choose different activities based on whether they want to relax, watch, direct, solve a problem, or build something.

When control and creation become entertainment

AI does not need to replace gaming to change how people spend their free time. It only needs to offer another loop that feels responsive, personal, and worth continuing.

For most people, that may mean watching generated media or directing finished systems. For a smaller group, it may mean opening an AI tool to build something instead of entering a world made by someone else.

That is the change I have noticed in myself. AI has made creation easier to begin and harder to separate from leisure. The output can be useful, but the attention loop still deserves the same scrutiny as any other form of entertainment.

The future of computer entertainment may include generated content to watch, intelligent systems to direct, and tools that make building something feel like play.
