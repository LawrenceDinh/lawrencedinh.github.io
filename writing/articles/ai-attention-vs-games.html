An hour that once went to a television show or game can now disappear into an AI session. The change is not that AI is automatically more entertaining. It is that creating something has become easy enough to enter casually.

This shift began before AI. Television brought professionally produced media into the home, while personal computers and consoles made interactive worlds widely accessible. Games arrived before creator-driven streaming because local hardware could respond immediately without relying on fast networks or practical video equipment.

Streaming later made publishing and on-demand viewing available to a much wider audience. AI lowers the barrier again: instead of choosing a finished program or entering one predefined world, the user can begin with a new goal. As intelligent software becomes cheaper, and if capable machines follow the same path, more leisure time may move from consuming finished experiences toward directing what happens.

Access changes the audience

Broadcast television gave large audiences access to the same professionally produced programs. That was a major expansion of access, but control remained concentrated among stations and production companies.

The viewer could choose a channel, but not change the program. The schedule, structure, and outcome had already been decided.

Home computers and consoles changed that relationship. Because games ran locally, interactive entertainment did not have to wait for broadband or online video platforms. The audience could act inside the experience and immediately see a response.

Online video required more infrastructure. Networks needed enough capacity to carry video reliably, while cameras and publishing tools needed to become practical for ordinary users. Once those barriers fell, streaming expanded both selection and participation.

The result is visible in current viewing habits. In a 2025 Pew Research Center survey, 83 percent of U.S. adults said they used streaming services, while 36 percent subscribed to cable or satellite television. [1]

Streaming gave viewers greater choice and content creators a more accessible way to publish. Most videos, however, still arrived as finished pieces. The audience could select the next item without changing the item itself.

Core shift

Each platform became easier to enter while giving the audience more influence over what happens next.

Games made agency normal

Games changed the audience from a viewer into an active participant. Progress depended on what the player did rather than on a sequence unfolding by itself.

The experience still existed inside a world created in advance. Its rules established what was possible, and those limits gave the player something stable to understand.

That structure is part of what makes games satisfying. The world responds, but it does not become an entirely different system whenever the player asks for something new.

Personal computers and consoles made this agency available without requiring players to create the underlying technology. Someone else built the world, but the player determined how the experience developed inside it.

Streaming expanded choice. Games changed the experience itself.

AI makes creation easier to enter

Generative AI is not confined to one predefined world. The user can begin with a new goal and describe it in ordinary language.

That distinction makes AI more open-ended than a conventional game. The system is not limited to one set of activities designed before the session began. It can produce a new result, receive feedback, and change direction.

A working session can move quickly from an idea to something visible. The user can inspect the result and clarify the direction without waiting for a separate production process.

Access to capable models has also become dramatically cheaper. Stanford’s 2025 AI Index reported that the cost of querying a system performing at roughly GPT-3.5 level fell from $20 per million tokens in November 2022 to $0.07 by October 2024. [2]

That decline matters because lower cost changes how casually the technology can be used. Experimenting with AI begins to feel less like starting a formal technical project and more like opening another form of interactive media.

Easier production is already increasing the amount of generated material entering existing platforms. TikTok reported in March 2026 that its systems had helped label more than 1.3 billion videos as AI-generated. [3]

Deezer reported the following month that it was receiving nearly 75,000 fully AI-generated tracks per day, representing about 44 percent of daily uploads. Those tracks received only a small share of total streams. [4]

The difference between uploads and attention is important. Making content easier to produce does not make every result worth watching.

AI competes on a different level. More people can attempt creation and revise the result before the session ends.

Why the loop keeps moving

A television episode usually provides an ending. Games divide play into objectives or sessions. AI often provides no equally clear stopping point because each result creates another decision.

A weak response presents a problem to solve. A strong response suggests another direction. Either result can make continuing feel reasonable.

The output also contains uncertainty. Better instructions may improve the next attempt without determining it completely.

Research on reward prediction error examines how differences between expected and received rewards contribute to learning. The research did not study AI interaction, so applying it here is an interpretation rather than direct evidence about AI use. [5]

Generative systems also produce meaningful variation between attempts. Research on creative tasks found that prompts can steer output while substantial variation still remains within the same model. [6]

The user therefore has enough control to guide the process, but not enough to know exactly what will appear next. That balance can make the session feel more active than a feed and less predictable than a conventional tool.

The session may also produce real work. That makes the attention loop harder to recognize, because useful output and unnecessary continuation can exist at the same time.

When making becomes leisure

Many projects lose momentum before anything useful appears. The first result may seem too far away, especially when unfamiliar tools stand between the idea and the work.

AI shortens that distance. A person can begin with ordinary language and reach a rough version while the original motivation is still active.

In my own projects, this can make building feel closer to play. I describe a direction, inspect the result, and decide what the next version should do.

The satisfaction does not come only from receiving an answer. It comes from watching an idea take form and influencing where it goes next.

This does not remove the need for technical understanding. A quick first version may still contain weak assumptions or fragile implementation. Easier access creates an earlier starting point, not automatic quality.

The larger change is cultural. Games brought agency into worlds created by someone else. AI lowers the threshold for producing a new result, allowing creation to become part of leisure itself.

As the cost and effort required to begin continue falling, some free time that once went toward consuming a finished experience may instead go toward making one.

When agency leaves the screen

Software can already receive a goal and choose digital actions. Robotics gives those actions a physical form.

For now, capable machines remain harder to access than AI running in a browser. They cost more, require physical space, and introduce risks that digital output does not.

There are early signs that the entry barrier may fall. Unitree currently lists its R1 humanoid robot from $4,900, although the platform remains an early product rather than a general-purpose household machine. [7]

The interaction can resemble controlling a character in a game. The user provides direction, observes the result, and responds to what happens next.

The difference is that the machine occupies shared physical space. A mistake can affect people or property instead of remaining inside a simulated world.

Working hypothesis

If useful machines become affordable and easy to direct, physical agency may become another casual use of free time.

This would continue the same accessibility pattern. A person would no longer need to design every movement or build the machine before directing what it does.

Physical agency could therefore feel like an extension of play, but with more responsibility. Greater access would need to arrive with stronger safety limits and better judgment.

The point is not that personal robots have already become mainstream entertainment. It is that falling cost and simpler control could eventually move agency from digital systems into the world around them.

Competing without replacing

Greater access and responsiveness do not make every newer medium better.

Finished television and film can provide deliberate pacing and a perspective that does not adapt to each viewer. Their fixed form allows a large audience to encounter the same work.

Games benefit from limits for a similar reason. The player has to understand the world rather than asking it to remove every obstacle.

AI offers more flexibility, but constant adaptation can weaken structure. A responsive system may struggle to provide the resistance or shared meaning found in authored work.

Productive use also deserves scrutiny. An AI session can leave behind something useful, but visible output does not make every additional hour valuable. The session may continue because another improvement feels close rather than because it still matters.

The larger pattern is widening access. Television brought professionally produced media into the home. Games made interactive worlds commonplace before online video infrastructure matured. Streaming later expanded who could publish, while AI lowers the barrier to creation itself.

Robotics may continue that pattern if capable machines become affordable and easier to direct. Some leisure time could move from watching finished experiences toward shaping digital output and physical action.

Earlier forms will remain valuable because they provide structure and shared experiences. AI introduces another possibility: entertainment can become a process of directing what happens next.

The next major platform may do more than show people something. It may give ordinary users a way to turn an idea into digital, and eventually physical, action.
